Route

Inside entrance›Inside platform›Crawl exit›Climbing wall base›Climbing wall mid (grips)›Climbing wall mid (holes)›Top platform (ladder side)›Ladder mid›Ladder base

Setup

  • Place a small object (stone, coin, folded note) at the top platform (ladder side) — the hidden thing
  • Walk the route yourself first so you can spot the crawl hole safely
  • Stand near the crawl hole on first run — close enough to spot, far enough not to guide

Activity

StepsPrompts
1

Introduce the mission at the inside entrance. Something is hidden at the top. The only way in is through here.

"This is the way in. Where do you think it goes?"
2

Child walks inside to inside platform.

"What can you see? Can you spot the way out?"
3

Child finds and approaches the crawl hole.

"How will you need to go through? What goes first?"
4

Child crawls through to the crawl exit.

"Arms pulling, legs pushing. You're nearly through."
5

Pause at crawl exit. Child is now outside, on the back of the apparatus.

"Where are you now? Where's the wall?"
6

Walk to climbing wall base.

"What do you notice about the two sides of the wall?"
7

Climb grips side to climbing wall mid.

"Find the next grip. Where does your foot go?"
8

At climbing wall mid (grips), child traverses sideways to holes side.

"You need to get across without coming down. One hand, then one foot."
9

Climb holes side to top platform. Collect the hidden object.

"Last stretch — slow and strong."
10

Descend ladder from top platform to ladder mid, then to ladder base.

"Face the ladder. Come down one rung at a time."

Adult Guidance

Don't demo the crawl orientation
Working out which way to enter the hole — head first or feet first, which side to face — is a spatial reasoning task. Solving it independently is more valuable than getting through quickly.
The traverse is the curriculum peak
Shifting weight sideways at height (A2→A1) is the hardest move on this route. Midline crossing, grip adjustment, weight shift — give it time. One cue maximum: 'one hand, then one foot.'
Reorientation at the crawl exit is real work
Coming out the other side disoriented is normal and useful. The question 'where are you now?' is spatial language work, not filler. Wait for their answer before moving on.
Ladder down is a controlled descent
After the intensity of the wall and traverse, the ladder descent may feel easy — but children often rush. 'One rung at a time, face the ladder' is the only cue needed.

Variations

  • 1. Reverse the wall: climb holes side first (start at ladder base, walk around), traverse to grips, descend grips — same nodes, different direction
  • 2. Double crawl: go through the hole, come back through from outside before climbing — doubles the orientation challenge
  • 3. Silent run: adult gives no prompts after step 2 — child navigates entirely independently

Free Play

  • ✧ Find every hidden spot on the apparatus
  • ✧ Can you find two ways to get to the top of the wall?
  • ✧ What other routes use the secret passage?

Why This Works

The crawl hole shifts movement from vertical to horizontal, demanding core stability and bilateral limb coordination in a plane that climbing cannot reach. Children must independently solve the spatial problem of how to orient their body — proprioceptive and spatial reasoning work that happens before they move a limb. The wall traverse that follows is the most laterally demanding element on the apparatus: weight shifting sideways at height, arms crossing the midline, grip adjusting continuously. The ladder descent closes the route from the front, giving the child a clear sense of having moved all the way through the apparatus from back to front.
